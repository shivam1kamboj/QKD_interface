Conclusion

In the Quantum Key Distribution (QKD) process, the presence of an eavesdropper, Eve, introduces discrepancies in the shared key between Alice and Bob due to the fundamental principles of quantum mechanics. When Eve attempts to measure the qubits being transmitted from Alice to Bob to gain information, the quantum states of these qubits are disturbed. This disturbance is a direct consequence of the no-cloning theorem and the quantum measurement process, where measuring a quantum state inherently alters it.

Because Alice and Bob select their measurement bases independently and randomly, any attempt by Eve to measure and resend the qubits in the same state they were originally (to avoid detection) is fundamentally flawed. Eve cannot perfectly clone the qubits due to the no-cloning theorem and has no knowledge of the bases Alice used to prepare them. Therefore, when Alice and Bob compare the results of their measurements in matching bases, they expect to see a 100% correlation in the absence of eavesdropping. However, because Eve's measurements cause state collapses in random bases, Alice and Bob will notice a higher error rate in their shared key where their bases matched.

This increased error rate is a clear indication of interference. By using privacy amplification and error correction protocols, Alice and Bob can further analyze the error rates to detect the presence of eavesdropping. If the error rate exceeds a certain threshold, they can conclude that the key distribution process has been compromised and can abort the process, ensuring the security of their communication. This demonstrates the power of QKD and its inherent protection against any form of eavesdropping, leveraging the laws of quantum mechanics to secure communication channels.
